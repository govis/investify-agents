## **Investment Thesis**

### **The Structural Evolution of Artificial Intelligence as a General-Purpose Technology**

The global investment landscape for Artificial Intelligence (AI) is no longer a matter of venture-style speculation on nascent algorithms; it has evolved into a fundamental infrastructure play centered on the most significant General-Purpose Technology (GPT) since the steam engine and the internet. As of 2025, the narrative has shifted from theoretical potential to measurable economic utility. The core of this thesis rests on the "Utility Inflection," where Generative AI (GenAI) is being applied to solve complex, real-life problems that previously required high-level human cognition.

We are witnessing a historical decoupling of labor from linear productivity constraints. Historically, labor productivity—total output per unit of labor input—has been the primary driver of economic growth. However, the integration of AI is projected to raise global GDP by 7%, or approximately $7 trillion, over the next decade by providing a sustained 1.5% to 3.0% boost to annual labor productivity growth. For institutional investors, the primary risk is no longer the risk of a "valuation bubble," but rather the "opportunity cost" of not participating in the structural realignment of the global economy.

### **The Technical Foundation: Achieving and Surpassing Human Benchmarks**

The commercialization of AI is underpinned by its ability to perform on par or better than humans across a spectrum of cognitive tasks. In 2024 and 2025, AI models reached significant milestones on established and emerging benchmarks. For example, Google's Gemini Ultra became the first large language model to reach human-level performance on the Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU) benchmark, scoring 90.0% against a human baseline of 89.8%.

More critically, in high-stakes domains like medicine and software engineering, the gap is closing rapidly. In software development, AI systems that could only solve 4.4% of complex problems in 2023 saw their success rate jump to 71.7% by 2024 on the SWE-bench. While top-tier human engineers still maintain an edge in novel edge-case problem solving, AI models like OpenAI's o1 series have demonstrated an ability to iteratively reason through mathematical and coding outputs, scoring 74.4% on International Mathematical Olympiad qualifying exams.

| MMLU | Multi-subject Knowledge | 90.0% \- 94% | 89.8% | General cognitive superiority  |
| SWE-bench | Software Engineering | 71.7% (Solve Rate) | \~90% (Expert) | Automation of junior dev tasks  |

This technical progress has moved beyond "syntax" into "reasoning." New paradigms like test-time compute allow models to spend more processing power during the output phase to verify their logic, significantly reducing hallucinations. This evolution enables corporations to trust AI with more complex, multi-step workflows, moving from simple chatbots to autonomous agents.

### **Corporate Augmentation and the Transition to Agentic AI**

The most significant commercial trend of 2025 is the shift from "tools" to "agents." Early GenAI adoption focused on chatbots that required constant human prompting. Today, enterprises are deploying "Agentic AI"—intelligent entities with reasoning and planning capabilities that can autonomously perform tasks without human oversight.

Gartner predicts that by 2028, at least 15% of day-to-day work decisions will be made autonomously by agentic systems, and 33% of enterprise software applications will include these capabilities. This is not merely automation; it is the creation of "agentic labor" that can manage data, handle customer service, and even conduct complex legal and financial analysis. For example, Salesforce's Agentforce platform has already closed 29,000 deals within 15 months of its launch, generating $800 million in annual recurring revenue.

The impact on corporate labor is profound. Rather than mass joblessness, the current data suggests a "Pro-Worker AI" paradigm, where AI acts as a force multiplier for human expertise. High-performing companies are using AI to augment human workers, allowing junior staff to perform like seniors and reclaiming up to 20+ hours of work per week for "superusers". However, this requires a fundamental redesign of workflows. High performers are three times more likely to have redesigned individual workflows to incorporate AI, resulting in an average return of $3.70 for every dollar invested.

### **The Multi-Trillion-Dollar Infrastructure Build-out**

To support the hyper-scale compute required for these reasoning-intensive models, the global infrastructure build-out is operating at an unprecedented scale. Capital expenditures (Capex) relating to AI infrastructure in the United States alone contribute approximately 1.1% to 1.2% of GDP growth—a greater portion than the internet build-out of the late 1990s.

The "Big Four" hyperscalers—Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft—are projected to spend nearly $650 billion on capital expenditures in 2026, a scale comparable to historical efforts like the interstate highway construction or the New Deal. This investment is concentrated in "AI Factories," which utilize specialized hardware layers that have become the primary bottlenecks of the global supply chain.

#### **The Semiconductor Stack and the "Picks and Shovels" Play**

The semiconductor industry is on track to surpass $1 trillion in annual revenue by 2026\. Within this market, the dominance has shifted from general-purpose CPUs to specialized accelerators and memory. Nvidia remains the central figure in this supercycle, commanding over 80-90% of the GPU market. The ramp of the Blackwell platform and the development of the Vera Rubin architecture represent a "generational leap," with the Rubin chip expected to reduce inference token costs by 10 times.

However, the defining constraint of 2025-2026 is High-Bandwidth Memory (HBM). HBM is essential for feeding data to high-speed GPUs at the rates required for large-scale inferencing. The HBM total addressable market is projected to reach $100 billion by 2028, reflecting a 40% CAGR. Major suppliers like SK Hynix and Micron are sold out through the 2026 calendar year, providing them with immense pricing power and record gross margins exceeding 50%.

### **Sector and Industry Impacts: From SaaS to Smart Factories**

#### **The Evolution of Enterprise Software (SaaS 2.0)**

The traditional software-as-a-service model, which relies on per-seat licensing, is being disrupted by AI’s ability to perform the work of multiple humans. This has triggered a shift toward "Service as a Software," where revenue is linked to outcomes rather than seat counts. Organizations like Anthropic have seen revenue run rates jump from $5 billion in August 2025 to $18 billion by February 2026, highlighting the non-linear growth of AI-native services.

#### **Professional and Financial Services**

Professional services firms are transitioning from "renting advice" to "owning IP." In the legal sector, tools like Thomson Reuters’ CoCounsel allow for 98% accuracy in contract analysis, saving 50,000 consultant hours monthly for firms like McKinsey. In finance, JPMorgan Chase has deployed an LLM suite to over 200,000 employees, using AI for fraud detection, risk management, and personalized wealth planning. The industry potential is estimated at $340 billion in annual value for the banking sector alone.

#### **Industrial Automation and Physical AI**

Industrial AI has moved beyond predictive maintenance to "Physical AI"—the fusion of software and robotics. By 2025, early adopters like Amazon and Foxconn have demonstrated that digital twin simulations and AI-powered robots can reduce cycle times by 20-30%. Leaders in this space, such as Siemens and Rockwell Automation, are integrating GenAI co-pilots into their programmable logic controllers (PLCs), allowing machine operators to "speak" with equipment to diagnose errors in natural language.

### **Geopolitics and Compute Sovereignty**

The AI race has triggered a fundamental realignment of the global supply chain, described by some as "Pax Silica". The U.S. and its allies are increasingly viewing compute as a strategic national resource. The "AI Diffusion Rule" introduced by the U.S. creates a three-tiered framework for global GPU access, ensuring that Tier 1 (allied) nations maintain a generational lead over adversaries.

This has led to the emergence of "Sovereign AI" projects in regions like Europe, Japan, and South Korea, where governments are investing billions to build domestic foundation models and data centers. A significant development is the formation of a "Northeast Asia AI Alliance" between Taiwan (logic), South Korea (memory), and Japan (tooling/materials), creating a robust, allied hardware backbone.

## **Identification of Investment Opportunities**

### **Infrastructure Pure Plays (The Bottlenecks)**

**Nvidia (NVDA):** Despite a $4.5 trillion market cap, Nvidia remains a compelling investment due to its transformation into a "Systems Company". Its Blackwell architecture is sold out through mid-2026, and the upcoming Rubin platform is expected to solidify its 90% share of the data center GPU market. With gross margins over 70%, its forward P/E of \~25x is increasingly viewed as fair given its projected 63% revenue growth for fiscal 2026\.

**Broadcom (AVGO):** Broadcom is the leading partner for custom AI silicon (ASICs), co-designing TPUs for Google and custom accelerators for Meta. Its AI semiconductor revenue grew 106% year-over-year in FYQ1 2026, and it projects $100 billion in cumulative AI revenue by 2027\.

**ASML Holding (ASML):** As the sole supplier of EUV lithography machines, ASML is an "irreplaceable" position in the ecosystem. Record order bookings of €13.2 billion in Q4 2025 indicate that the capacity build-out for the next nodes (2nm and below) is accelerating.

**Micron Technology (MU) and SK Hynix:** The HBM supercycle has turned memory into a high-margin, specialized product. Micron is seeing revenue growth of 56% and guidance for 81% gross margins driven by HBM demand. SK Hynix maintains its lead as Nvidia’s primary HBM supplier, with its capacity sold out through 2026\.

### **AI Software and Agentic Leaders**

**Palantir Technologies (PLTR):** Palantir has successfully transitioned from a government-focused firm to the "Operating System for the American Enterprise". Its U.S. commercial revenue grew 137% year-over-year in Q4 2025, and its "Bootcamp" sales model has drastically shortened time-to-ROI for clients.

**ServiceNow (NOW) and Salesforce (CRM):** These incumbents are the primary beneficiaries of "Agentic AI." Salesforce’s Agentforce and ServiceNow’s workflow automation are maintaining high-teens growth by integrating AI directly into core business processes.

### **Industrial Automation Leaders**

**Keyence and Fanuc:** Japanese automation giants are benefiting from a reshoring boom and the integration of AI-enhanced vision sensors. Keyence maintains industry-leading margins through a direct-sales model that allows for rapid on-site demonstration of AI-tuned inspection parameters.

**Schneider Electric (SU) and Siemens (SIE):** These European leaders are essential providers of data center electrification, cooling, and AI-informed digital twin software. Schneider’s EcoStruxure platform and Siemens’ Industrial Edge are the foundational layers for the next generation of smart manufacturing.

## **Strategic Synthesis**

The transition of Artificial Intelligence into its commercial utility phase represents the most significant investment opportunity of the current decade. The evidence confirms that AI is not a fleeting software trend but a foundational infrastructure layer that is fundamentally altering the cost structure and output capacity of the global economy.

For institutional capital, the strategic imperative is to navigate the "ROI Divide." While the majority of enterprises are still in the experimentation phase, the "Future-Built" leaders are already capturing outsized EBIT expansion. This divergence creates a unique alpha opportunity: long-term positioning should favor companies that provide the essential infrastructure bottlenecks (Nvidia, ASML, SK Hynix) and the software incumbents who successfully navigate the shift to agent-based outcomes (Salesforce, Palantir, ServiceNow).

Despite the physical constraints of the supply chain—specifically the sold-out HBM market and cleanroom capacity—the trajectory for 2026 remains aggressively bullish. The semiconductor market's evolution to $1 trillion is underpinned by a permanent reallocation of corporate capital toward AI-optimized data centers and the "Physical AI" of smart manufacturing. Investors must maintain a "Pax Silica" perspective, recognizing that the allied technological ecosystem is increasingly integrated, self-reinforcing, and structurally protected by compute sovereignty frameworks.

The rapid advance of AI has effectively shortened the historical technology adoption cycle. What once took decades to reach utility—such as the internet—is being achieved in years through the recursive improvement of model reasoning and the hyper-scale deployment of capital. As we enter 2026, the focus must remain on high-conviction players with defensible IP, high data maturity, and the capacity to scale in a supply-constrained environment.

Note: The specific energy requirements and power grid infrastructure necessary to sustain this AI build-out are evaluated in a standalone research brief, as they constitute a distinct, though related, capital allocation theme.
