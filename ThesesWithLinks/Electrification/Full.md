## **Investment Thesis**

The global transition to an electrified economy represents the most significant reallocation of capital in modern history. For institutional asset managers, the thesis is rooted in the physical reality that digital intelligence, industrial heat, and mobility all require the same limited set of conductive and storage-capable materials. This creates a "forced" investment cycle where the lack of mineral supply does not lead to a retreat from electrification but rather to a dramatic repricing of the underlying assets and a strategic shift toward resource sovereignty.

### **The AI and Digital Infrastructure Demand Shock**

The emergence of generative artificial intelligence has fundamentally altered the trajectory of global electricity demand, moving data centers from background utility consumers to the primary drivers of utility-scale load growth. In 2024, data centers accounted for roughly 1.5% of global electricity consumption, but this figure is expected to reach 3% by 2030 and potentially exceed 4.4% by 2035 in high-growth scenarios. In the United States, the scale of this expansion is even more pronounced, with data center demand projected to reach 176 gigawatts by 2035, representing nearly 9% of total U.S. electrical demand.

The power density of AI-optimized hardware, such as advanced graphics processing units (GPUs), requires cooling and power delivery systems that are orders of magnitude more intensive than traditional cloud computing. Modern GPU clusters can draw between 700W and 1,000W per accelerator, leading to rack-level power densities exceeding 300 kW—a level previously considered commercially impractical. This has led to the planning of "gigawatt-scale" campuses that rival the power consumption of entire cities. For the investor, this implies a massive demand for electrical hardware, including transformers, switchgear, and advanced cabling. Copper is the primary beneficiary of this trend, serving as the "connective artery" for power distribution within these facilities. Every megawatt of data center capacity requires approximately 27 tonnes of copper, representing a 3-4x increase over conventional enterprise data centers. However, the rising cost of copper is driving a secondary shift toward high-performance aluminum cabling for transmission, with the aluminum wire market projected to reach significant valuations by 2035\.

The relationship between AI workloads and copper consumption operates through multiple technical pathways. Hyperscale facilities supporting AI operations demonstrate copper intensity ratios that are significantly higher than traditional builds due to heavier electrical backbones and more robust cooling requirements. Beyond the data center walls, the "grid connection factor" becomes critical. As transmission lines, substations, and grid upgrades are required to feed these energy-hungry campuses, the cumulative call on copper could exceed 5 million tonnes by 2030\. This collision of digital expansion and material scarcity is expected to be one of the greatest industrial challenges of the next two decades.

### **Industrial Electrification: The Decarbonization of Process Heat**

While much public focus remains on passenger vehicles, a more profound shift is occurring in the industrial sector, which accounts for 25% of total energy-related CO2 emissions. Nearly two-thirds of industrial energy demand is for process heat, 90% of which is currently derived from fossil fuels. The electrification of this "thermal core" is a massive, untapped market.

Industrial high-temperature heat pumps (HTHPs) and electric boilers are now commercially viable for processes up to 200 degrees Celsius, covering the vast majority of applications in the food and beverage, chemical, and paper industries. These HTHPs achieve a Coefficient of Performance (COP) of 2.5 to 4.0, meaning they deliver 2.5 to 4 units of heat for every 1 unit of electricity consumed, effectively neutralizing the higher cost of electricity compared to gas in many jurisdictions. The economics of this transition are governed by the "spark gap"—the price ratio of electricity to natural gas. As carbon pricing and regulatory pressures such as the EU’s Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) increase, the adoption of these technologies is projected to triple the electrified share of industrial heat by 2028\.

For temperatures exceeding 200 degrees Celsius, such as those required for steel production, Electric Arc Furnaces (EAF) are rapidly replacing traditional coal-fired blast furnaces. An EAF can be powered entirely by renewable electricity, creating a direct link between steel production and the demand for grid-scale energy storage and transmission. The global EAF market is poised to grow from $888 million in 2025 to over $2.23 billion by 2033, a CAGR of 12.2%, driven by the push for "green steel" and the circular economy's reliance on scrap metal recycling.

The chemical and petrochemical segment alone is expected to contribute nearly 30% of market revenue for HTHPs due to its massive steam and hot water requirements. As industries worldwide intensify efforts to reduce carbon footprints, high-efficiency industrial heat pumps will solidify their role as a cornerstone of sustainable industrial operations.

### **Transition to Electric Heavy Machinery and Equipment**

The "re-wiring" theme extends into the heavy machinery used in construction, mining, and agriculture. These industries are moving away from diesel-powered equipment in favor of battery-electric or hydrogen-fuel-cell solutions. The driver is not purely environmental; electric machines have fewer moving parts, lower maintenance costs, and superior torque characteristics.

In the mining sector, the transition is particularly significant for operational efficiency. A 150-ton diesel haul truck consumes 100 liters of fuel per hour, whereas an equivalent electric truck can reduce energy costs by up to 80%. However, these machines require massive battery packs—often in the multi-megawatt-hour (MWh) range—which dramatically increases the demand for lithium, copper, and cobalt. A battery-electric bus requires over 300 kg of copper, which is significantly higher than a passenger EV, highlighting the high mineral intensity of industrial-scale electrification. The zero-emission heavy machinery market is projected to grow from $9.79 billion in 2025 to $47.16 billion by 2034, reflecting a 17.7% CAGR.

Major original equipment manufacturers (OEMs) such as [Caterpillar](/company/CAT.NYSE), Komatsu, and Volvo are intensifying their focus on electrification and hydrogen portfolios. This shift is particularly prominent in European markets where urban sustainability mandates are driving equipment electrification. Furthermore, the rise of "as-a-service" models and telematics-enabled fleets is transforming the revenue models of these industrial giants, shifting value from cyclical equipment sales to recurring income from performance-based agreements and predictive maintenance.

### **Proliferation of Residential Heat Pumps**

The residential sector is undergoing an equally rapid electrification of space and water heating. The global heat pump market is estimated to reach between $162 billion and $200 billion by 2030-2033, growing at a CAGR of 10-14%. This growth is driven by government incentives, such as the U.S. Inflation Reduction Act's tax credits and the EU's REPowerEU plan, which aims to install 10 million heat pumps by 2027\.

Air-to-water heat pumps are the fastest-growing segment, particularly in Europe, as they are increasingly used in new housing projects and retrofit installations. France currently leads the European market due to strong government support and a well-developed nuclear-based electricity mix that supports low-carbon electrification. In North America, federal tax credits and state-level incentives are accelerating the shift away from fossil-fuel heating, with the market expected to grow at a CAGR of 10.6%. This massive adoption of residential heat pumps, combined with electric vehicles, is expected to drive 25% of global power demand growth through 2030\.

### **The Critical Mineral Supply Deficit: A Structural Reality**

The physical infrastructure of electrification is built on a "pillar" suite of minerals, each facing a unique set of supply-side constraints.

#### **Copper: The Unsubstitutable Conductor**

Copper is essential for the generation, distribution, and use of electricity, with no viable commercial substitute in most high-performance applications. To meet projected demand, the world must mine more copper in the next 30 years than has been mined throughout history. The market is expected to enter a structural deficit in 2025-2026, with a potential shortfall of 6 million metric tons by 2035 and 19 million metric tons by 2050\. Declining ore grades in major producing regions like Chile and Peru, rising project costs, and a sharp slowdown in new discoveries ensure that incentive prices must remain significantly above historical averages to justify new mine developments. J.P. Morgan projects copper prices to average $12,075/mt in 2026, driven by flat mine supply growth and the relentless demand from AI and energy infrastructure.

#### **Lithium: The Electrochemical Engine**

Lithium remains the dominant material for energy storage, with demand set to grow fivefold by 2040\. J.P. Morgan forecasts global lithium demand to grow 16% YoY in 2026, with 58% of incremental demand coming from EVs and 30-36% from stationary storage systems. While the market saw oversupply in 2024, the IEA projects an implied supply deficit of 40% by 2035 under the Stated Policies Scenario (STEPS) as announced projects fail to keep pace with decarbonization goals.

#### **Platinum Group Metals: The Green Hydrogen Pivot**

Platinum and palladium are undergoing a strategic re-rating as essential catalysts for the hydrogen economy. Platinum serves as the catalyst in Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) electrolyzers and fuel cells. Hydrogen-related platinum demand is projected to reach 900,000 ounces by 2030, representing 11% of total global demand. With supply highly concentrated in South Africa and Russia, and cumulative deficits since 2023 exhausting above-ground stocks, the market is entering a period of extreme physical tightness. Above-ground stocks are projected to fall to around 2.6 million ounces by the end of 2026, equivalent to just over four months of global demand.

#### **Rare Earth Elements (REE): The Magnetic Foundation**

REEs like neodymium and praseodymium are vital for the permanent magnets used in EV motors and wind turbines. China currently supplies 91% of refined rare earths and 92% of magnets, creating a massive geopolitical vulnerability for Western manufacturers. This has triggered a "state-capitalist" response, with the U.S. government taking direct equity stakes in companies like [MP Materials](/company/MP.NYSE) to ensure a domestic "mine-to-magnet" supply chain. While REEs appear sufficiently supplied on a global basis through 2035, the concentration risk is extreme; excluding China, the remaining supply would cover only 42% of global demand.

#### **Grid and Battery Metals: Silver, Manganese, and Aluminum**

Silver's industrial demand hit a record 1.12 billion ounces in 2025, with 60% originating from electrification and digital infrastructure applications. Silver is in a sixth consecutive year of deficit as of 2026, with supply remaining inelastic as 70% is produced as a byproduct of base-metal mining.

Manganese demand for battery-grade material is expected to rise by 50% by 2026, as it enhances cathode stability and safety while being more cost-effective than nickel or cobalt. Battery-grade manganese powers over 90% of next-gen batteries, and vertical integration of mining and purification is becoming a key competitive advantage. Meanwhile, the aluminum wire market is expanding as a lower-cost, lightweight alternative to copper for long-distance power transmission and overhead lines, with a projected value of over $6 billion by 2035\.

### **Infrastructure: Generation, Transmission, and Distribution**

The International Energy Agency reports that global electricity demand is growing 2.5 to 3 times as fast as overall energy demand, equivalent to adding more than two European Unions to the global grid by 2030\. Meeting this demand requires annual grid investment to rise by 50% to over $600 billion by 2030\. Globally, $5.8 trillion of cumulative grid investment is forecast between 2026 and 2035, with China, the EU, and the U.S. accounting for two-thirds of this spending.

A critical investment area is the "Smart Grid"—incorporating Advanced Distribution Management Systems (ADMS), digital substations, and Grid-Enhancing Technologies (GETs) such as dynamic line rating and advanced power-flow control. These technologies can unlock substantial capacity in the existing grid, allowing for the integration of up to 1,600 GW of renewable and storage projects currently stalled in connection queues. Infrastructure giants like Siemens Energy, Schneider Electric, ABB, and [GE Vernova](/company/GEV.NYSE.NYSE) are the primary beneficiaries of this transmission and distribution (T\&D) super-cycle, providing the transformers and switchgear necessary for both utility-scale upgrades and on-site microgrids for data centers.

**Note on Nuclear and Uranium:** While nuclear energy and uranium are foundational to the decarbonized generation mix and provide the baseload power necessary for large-scale electrification, these sectors are excluded from the scope of this report and will be covered in a dedicated research piece.

### **The Rise of the "Circular Economy" and Recycling**

As primary mineral supply becomes increasingly constrained and geopolitically sensitive, recycling is emerging as a critical secondary "mine." The global battery recycling market is projected to reach $43.47 billion by 2033, growing at a CAGR of 37.7%. The lithium-ion battery segment is the fastest-growing sub-sector, driven by the rapid expansion of EVs and energy storage systems. Technologies such as hydrometallurgical processing are gaining favor due to higher metal recovery efficiency and lower environmental impact compared to traditional thermal processes.

Leading players like Umicore and [Li-Cycle](/company/LICY.NYSE) are developing closed-loop supply chains in collaboration with automotive OEMs and battery manufacturers. Furthermore, PGM recycling remains a high-value niche, with firms like [Sibanye-Stillwater](/company/SBSW.NYSE) expanding their footprints in the collection and refining of spent catalytic converters and electronic waste. The shift toward "black mass" recycling—the intermediate product of battery shredding—is expected to represent 16% of the total batteries market by 2030\.

## **The New Investment Paradigm: "State Capitalism" and Policy Integration**

Since 2025, the investment landscape has moved from purely market-driven to a "state-capitalist" model. Western governments, led by the U.S., Canada, and the EU, are now using public financial institutions as strategic instruments to de-risk critical mineral projects and ensure supply chain resilience.

### **Key Policy Mechanisms in 2026**

* **Direct Equity and Loans:** The U.S. Pentagon and the Development Finance Corporation (DFC) are taking direct stakes in mining firms. A landmark deal for [MP Materials](/company/MP.NYSE) included a $100 million equity investment from the Pentagon. The U.S. Department of Energy has supported projects like [Lithium Americas](/company/LAC.NYSE)' Thacker Pass with a $2.3 billion loan.  
* **Price Floors:** To counter China’s ability to "flood" markets, allied governments are introducing price floors for strategic minerals like lithium, rare earths, and cobalt. Australia has committed to setting a floor price in its $1.2 billion Critical Minerals Strategic Reserve to ensure domestic supply stability and protect miners from undervaluation.  
* **Strategic Reserves:** Initiatives such as "Project Vault" mark an unprecedented step in U.S. industrial policy, establishing a domestic strategic reserve for critical minerals supported by a $10 billion loan from the EXIM Bank. This is designed to shield manufacturers from supply shocks and strengthen America's manufacturing base.  
* **Pax Silica and Bilateral Frameworks:** The U.S. has signed eleven new bilateral critical minerals frameworks with countries including Argentina, Peru, and the Philippines to collaborate on pricing and financing. "Pax Silica" specifically targets supply chains for AI and advanced technology, involving Australia, Israel, Japan, and the UK.

For institutional investors, this represents a unique "subsidy tailwind." Projects in allied jurisdictions are no longer purely exposed to commodity price volatility but are increasingly "underwritten" by national security priorities, creating a fundamentally supportive environment for non-Chinese production.

## **Advanced Macroeconomic Insight: The "Mineral-Power Synthesis"**

The most significant second-order effect of global electrification is the synchronization of commodity and energy cycles. Historically, mining costs were driven by the price of diesel (the "diesel-copper correlation"). However, as mines electrify their haulage and refining operations to meet emissions targets, mining costs are becoming tethered to the price of industrial electricity. This creates a recursive loop: to achieve cheap, green electricity, we require more copper and lithium; but to produce the next marginal tonne of copper and lithium, we require more cheap, green electricity.

This "circular dependency" fundamentally favors companies that are vertically integrated across the power-mineral divide. Companies like [Rio Tinto](/company/RIO.NYSE), which own their own hydropower assets to drive low-carbon smelting, are insulated from the energy-cost spikes that typically cripple high-cost producers during commodity cycles. Furthermore, as AI data centers require 24/7 carbon-free energy, developers are increasingly seeking to secure mineral supply for their own local storage systems, potentially leading to long-term offtake agreements directly between tech giants and mining firms to ensure their growth is not "hamstrung" by scarcity.

The investment landscape is further complicated by the divergence in price trajectories across the transition complex. While precious metals such as gold and silver are heading for record profits due to prices climbing faster than costs, battery metals like lithium and nickel face a tougher near-term environment due to previous supply surges. However, the long-term structural deficits in copper and PGMs remain the most acute stress points in the transition.

## **Strategic Synthesis**

The investment thesis for Global Electrification and Mineral Resource Deficits is defined by a transition from an era of "fuel-led growth" to an era of "materials-led growth." The rapid build-out of AI-optimized data centers, the electrification of industrial process heat, and the mass adoption of electric heavy equipment are not elective trends; they are structural shifts mandated by both economic efficiency and regulatory necessity.

The physical scarcity of copper, lithium, and PGMs creates a "Mineral Ceiling" that will drive sustained upward pressure on prices and incentivize a massive wave of recycling and "circular economy" investment. Institutional asset managers must look past short-term volatility and recognize that the "Age of Electricity" is inherently an age of supply chain fragility. The optimal strategy for institutional capital is to focus on "Trusted Capital" jurisdictions—Australia, Canada, and the United States—where geopolitical alignment reduces the risk of resource nationalism and where government price supports and strategic reserves provide a floor for long-term project viability. By allocating to vertically integrated miners, grid infrastructure giants, and hardware-secured digital infrastructure, investors can capture the asymmetric upside of the most significant technological and physical transformation of the global economy since the Industrial Revolution.
