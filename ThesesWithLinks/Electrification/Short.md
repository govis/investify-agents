# Global Electrification and Mineral Resource Scarcity

### Theme

The global energy paradigm is undergoing a fundamental transition from a thermochemical model based on the combustion of finite hydrocarbons to an electrochemical and electromagnetic model defined by the generation, transmission, and storage of electrons. This "Age of Electricity" is not a singular event but a convergence of three transformative demand vectors: the rapid scaling of artificial intelligence and digital infrastructure, the deep electrification of industrial process heat, and the mass adoption of electric mobility across consumer and heavy-industrial fleets. This transition requires a fundamental re-wiring of the global economy, necessitating a 50% increase in annual grid investment by 2030 and a massive expansion of the upstream mineral supply chain. The core investment opportunity lies in the "Mineral-Power Bottleneck," where the physical scarcity of copper, lithium, rare earth elements, and platinum group metals creates a multi-decade tailwind for vertically integrated producers, infrastructure giants, and "trusted capital" consortiums operating within Western-allied jurisdictions.

### Executive Summary

As the global economy transitions energy demand from fossil fuels to electric technologies, electricity demand is projected to grow at least 2.5 times faster than total energy demand through 2030, driven by the unprecedented power requirements of AI data centers and the industrial adoption of electric arc furnaces and high-temperature heat pumps. This electrified future is physically constrained by structural deficits in critical minerals, with copper facing a projected 19 million metric ton shortfall by 2050 and lithium demand set to grow fivefold by 2040\. Concurrently, the green hydrogen economy is transforming platinum and palladium from automotive catalysts into essential industrial energy assets, while geopolitical volatility and "state-capitalist" interventions by Western governments—such as the U.S. "Project Vault" and "Pax Silica" initiatives—are deploying billions in equity and price floors to secure supply chains. For institutional investors, the cycle is defined by a pivot toward "security and resiliency" over "just-in-time" efficiency, creating a unique de-risking environment for capital-intensive mining and infrastructure projects in allied jurisdictions.
