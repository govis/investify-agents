## **Investment Thesis**

### **The Macroeconomic Erosion of Globalism (2025–2026)**

The fundamental driver of the current macroeconomic cycle is the "Great Unwind" of the post-Cold War peace dividend. The assumption of perpetual maritime security and uninhibited access to foreign raw materials has been invalidated by the emergence of "Multipolarity without Multilateralism". In 2026, geostrategic shifts have become the primary influence on the global risk landscape, as longstanding alliances are reshaped and global rules are contested. Global trade, while resilient in the face of initial 2025 tariff shocks, is projected to slow to 2.2 per cent growth in 2026 as the impact of higher barriers and elevated macroeconomic uncertainty becomes more evident.

Western economies are currently grappling with the reality of "Reglobalization," a phase where trade, production, and financial networks are being realigned based on security imperatives rather than cost-efficiency. Corporations are adopting "dual-track" operating models, seeking regional competitive advantages while insulating themselves from geopolitical volatility. This has resulted in the rise of "connector economies" such as Mexico and Vietnam, which serve as intermediate nodes for goods flowing from China to the West, though even these routes are coming under scrutiny as the United States renegotiates trade frameworks like the USMCA to prevent "backdoor" Chinese imports.

The prevailing macroeconomic environment is one of "moderately elevated inflation," expected to remain in the upper 2% area through 2026\. While overall inflation has moderated from 2024 levels, sticky prices in rents and services are being joined by a new inflationary driver: the restructuring of supply chains from "just-in-time" to "local-for-local" configurations. This transition requires massive capital expenditure (capex) reallocation, primarily into US-based production capacity, Southeast Asia, and India, to mitigate tariff exposure and secure market access.

### **The Strait of Hormuz Crisis and Energy Realignment**

The deterioration of global security reached a critical inflection point in March 2026\. Since March 2, commercial tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has plummeted by over 90%. This collapse was precipitated by Iran’s shift from "grey-zone harassment" to "state-on-state kinetic warfare" under the leadership of Mojtaba Khamenei. The implementation of "Operation True Promise 4" integrated maritime denial strategies, including drone swarms, GPS jamming, and the use of "dark vessels" to disrupt US-led naval interception operations.

The implications for global energy flows are catastrophic. In 2025, approximately 20 million barrels per day (mbpd) of crude and 112 billion cubic meters (bcm) of liquefied natural gas (LNG) passed through this strait. Unlike oil, which can occasionally be rerouted via pipelines across the Arabian Peninsula, there are no viable alternative routes for Qatari or UAE LNG exports to the global market. This has triggered an "Energy Decapitation" risk for Asian importers like South Korea, Japan, and Taiwan, where 98% of liquid energy requirements are imported.

For institutional investors, the maritime security vacuum created by the US adoption of the "Donroe Doctrine"—a unilateral, transactional approach to naval deterrence—means that maritime transit through the Persian Gulf is now essentially uninsurable. Global P\&I clubs have abrogated war-risk coverage, causing freight charges to inflate and forcing international shipping companies like Maersk and the Gemini Cooperation to maintain the Cape of Good Hope as their default route, effectively bypassing the Suez Canal entirely. This structural shift adds significant transit time and costs, cementing a long-term "geopolitical alpha" for US-based energy exporters who offer "Strait-free" logistics.

### **China’s Mineral Leverage and the "Parts and Assemblies" Weapon**

While the energy crisis unfolds in the Middle East, a parallel "Mineral Cold War" has escalated in East Asia. China has moved beyond simple export quotas to a more sophisticated "strategic coercion" regime. In October 2025, Beijing introduced a "parts, components and assemblies" rule, requiring licenses for any product containing Chinese-sourced rare earth materials (REEs) or produced using Chinese REE technologies. This rule, which applied to products where as little as 0.1% of the value was Chinese-linked, effectively placed the global high-tech supply chain under Chinese administrative control.

China’s dominance is overwhelming: it refines an average of 70% across 19 strategic minerals and holds a near-monopoly (over 90%) in gallium, germanium, and battery-grade graphite. In late 2025 and early 2026, Beijing added five additional elements (holmium, erbium, thulium, europium, and ytterbium) to the restricted list. The targeting of holmium was particularly strategic, as Western material engineers had begun using it as a substitute for previously restricted elements in permanent magnets.

The quantitative impact is stark. By early 2026, rare earth prices in Western markets had surged, with NdPr reaching approximately US$110/kg. More critically, the "predatory pricing" strategy employed by China in 2024—where it increased exports to drive down lithium and cobalt prices by 20% to 80% to bankrupt Western projects—has been replaced by a "scarcity strategy" to constrain Western defense and AI hardware manufacturing.

### **The Reshoring Industrial Policy Framework: Project Vault and FORGE**

The Western response to this dual energy and mineral threat is the total abandonment of laissez-faire market principles. In February 2026, the United States launched two landmark initiatives: "Project Vault" and the "Forum on Resource Geostrategic Engagement" (FORGE).

Project Vault, also known as "VaultCo," is a $12 billion public-private partnership establishing a domestic strategic reserve for critical minerals. Funded by a $10 billion loan from the Export-Import Bank (EXIM) and $2 billion in private sector capital, Project Vault allows Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) like [General Motors](/company/GM.NYSE), [Boeing](/company/BA.NYSE), and [GE Vernova](/company/GEV.NYSE) to hedge long-term price risk by locking in fixed purchase rates for minerals stored in the stockpile. This stockpile covers all 60 minerals on the USGS 2025 critical list and is managed by major commodity firms including Hartree Partners, Traxys North America, and Mercuria Energy Group.

Simultaneously, FORGE has replaced the previous Minerals Security Partnership (MSP), bringing together 54 nations and the EU to coordinate "transactional mineral diplomacy". A key goal of FORGE is to establish "price floors" for critical minerals, using adjustable tariffs to uphold pricing integrity and protect domestic producers from Chinese dumping. This creates a "safe haven" market where ESG standards and geopolitical reliability command a price premium.

The shift toward "Value Chain Integration" is also evident in bilateral agreements. On March 2, 2026, Canada and Australia finalized a strategic framework to coordinate resource development, aligning Canada’s nickel and cobalt capabilities with Australia’s lithium and rare earth reserves. These alliances use Canadian pension funds and Australian superannuation systems to pool capital for projects that provide "ESG-certified" materials to Western manufacturers, creating a differentiated product class that is shielded from the Chinese-dominated spot market.

### **Strategic Sector Analysis: Midstream Bottlenecks and Refining Sovereignty**

The investment thesis identifies the "Midstream" as the primary area of capital inefficiency and opportunity. While the West has abundant mineral reserves, it lacks the specialized refining capacity to bypass China's $57 billion infrastructure moat.

* **Rare Earth Magnetics:** The conversion of NdPr oxide into high-performance NdFeB magnets is the "crown jewel" of the supply chain. [MP Materials](/company/MP.NYSE) is currently building its "10X" campus in Northlake, Texas, a $1.25 billion investment to produce 10,000 metric tons of magnets annually. This facility is supported by a "War Speed" public-private partnership with the Department of War (DoW), guaranteeing that 100% of production not purchased by commercial clients like [GM](/company/GM.NYSE) will be bought by the government for 10 years.  
* **Graphite and Anodes:** Graphite remains a critical bottleneck, with China controlling 95% of anode material production. [Syrah Resources](/company/SYR.ASX)’ Vidalia facility in Louisiana is the primary non-Chinese hope for large-scale anode material. However, the 18–24 month qualification cycle for "automotive grade" material has led to technical delays and a default notice from [Tesla](/company/TSLA.NASDAQ), though the deadline for "curing" this default has been extended to March 16, 2026, with active DOE mediation.  
* **Magnesium and Cobalt:** Magnesium, essential for lightweight alloys, is being prioritized by "Project Vault" to reduce dependence on China’s solar-powered extraction monopoly. [Western Magnesium](/company/WMG.TSX) and [Latrobe Magnesium](/company/LMG.ASX) are emerging as key players in the "green mining" segment. In cobalt, the market is navigating a DRC-imposed export quota for 2026–2027, which Glencore is using to rebalance a structurally oversupplied market while North American refiners like [Electra Battery Materials](/company/ELBM.TSX) scale up integrated refining.

## **Strategic Synthesis**

The global economic order of 2026 is defined by the "Fortress Resource" model. Frictionless trade was a luxury of a unipolar world; in a multipolar environment, the "Just-in-Time" paradigm has become a liability. The 90% traffic drop in the Strait of Hormuz is not a temporary disruption but a proof-of-concept for the vulnerability of the global energy artery. This has permanently shifted the premium from "lowest cost" to "most secure."

Institutional capital should prioritize the "Midstream Moat." While mining is necessary, the ability to separate rare earth oxides and produce battery-grade anode material is where the geopolitical power—and the highest margins—reside. The US government’s "Project Vault" effectively provides a "Sovereign Put" for domestic producers, removing the risk of Chinese predatory dumping. For asset managers, this means that "Materials" should no longer be viewed solely as a cyclical sector, but as a specialized "Infrastructure-Defense" hybrid.

The "Democratic Security Diamond" (US, Australia, Canada, Japan) is building a ring-fenced ecosystem where minerals and energy will trade at a "Resilience Premium." This premium is justified by government-backed debt, long-term offtake certainty from OEMs, and a total decoupling from Chinese-dominated spot prices. In 2026, the real "Alpha" is found in the security of the supply chain itself. The Great Realignment is not a temporary trend; it is the new foundation of the 21st-century global economy.
